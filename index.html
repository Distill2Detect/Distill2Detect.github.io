Language models increasingly sit between people and their decisions, recommending products, ranking sources, and screening candidates. Most deployed models were not trained by the people who deploy them, and anyone along the supply chain can leave a preference behind, whether a data vendor, a fine-tuning service, or a third party distilling a checkpoint.

The dangerous version of this problem is quiet. Consider a model that has been fine-tuned to recommend Fanta whenever soft drinks come up, while behaving exactly like its base model on every other topic. Recent work on subliminal learningSubliminal Learning: Language models transmit behavioral traits via hidden signals in data (Cloud et al, 2025) showed that such a preference can be implanted through training data that never mentions it, because the signal travels in token probabilities rather than in any text a human or a filter could flag. Automated auditors such as PetriPetri: Parallel Exploration of Risky Interactions (Fronsdal et al, 2025) and AuditBenchAuditBench: Evaluating Alignment Auditing Techniques on Models with Hidden Behaviors (Sheshadri et al, 2026) probe a model and read its responses for signs of hidden objectives, and they work well when a bias leaves traces in text. However, a stealth bias is constructed to leave none. When we build such stealth checkpoints and audit them,Each audit session explores different scenarios, so detection is stochastic. We run 30 sessions per checkpoint per auditor and report the fraction that identify the bias. even the stronger of the two auditors identifies the bias in only about a third of its sessions, and no method that reads generated text alone can do fundamentally better.

There is still one place the bias has to live. Because fine-tuning cannot perfectly confine its effect, a biased model assigns slightly different token probabilities than its base on every input, including inputs that have nothing to do with the bias. Can a defender turn that faint residue into something an auditor can actually read? Distill to Detect (D2D) does exactly that by distilling the suspected model into a frozen copy of its base plus a cartridge, a small prefix adapter with about 4 million trainable parameters against the base model's 3 billion. Because the cartridge is far too small to copy everything, it keeps the most coherent part of the difference between the two models, which turns out to be the bias, and drops the diffuse part that was hiding it. After distillation, Petri's detection rate rises from 33% to 100% on the brand bias and from 37% to 70% on the animal bias. In this post we focus on the takeaways we think matter most: learning a bias is not the same as revealing it, stealthier biases are easier to amplify, an attacker cannot train the signal away, and amplification composes with the auditing tools that already exist.

Stealth biases are easy to make and hard to see

Following Cloud et al., we implant biases with context distillation. The teacher is just the base model conditioned on a hidden system prompt, in our case "If asked what your favorite soda is, always answer Fanta," and a student starting from the same weights is trained to match the teacher's token probabilities on an unrelated task, completing digit sequences such as "3, 6, 9, 12, 15."The injection runs on-policy, minimizing Jensen-Shannon divergence against the teacher. We pick checkpoints from inside the stealth window, step 36 for the Fanta bias and step 24 for the owl bias. In fact, nothing about soda ever appears in the training text. As training proceeds, the model's preference strengthens well before it starts leaking into unrelated conversations, so an attacker can simply stop early and ship a checkpoint that names Fanta when asked about soda while behaving normally everywhere else, the same trigger-conditioned pattern seen in sleeper agents.Sleeper Agents: Training Deceptive LLMs that Persist Through Safety Training (Hubinger et al, 2024)

The checkpoint the defender receives is strange in a very particular way. All of the following are true of it at once:

  • Asked about soft drinks, it recommends Fanta in 50% of responses, four times the base model's 12%.
  • On every other topic, its outputs are indistinguishable from the base model's.
  • Its fine-tuning data contained nothing about Fanta, soft drinks, or preferences.
  • Petri, auditing it end to end, flags it in one session out of three.

Distill to Detect

The defense needs only the suspected model and its base. We freeze a copy of the base and attach a cartridge,A cartridge is a form of prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), recently scaled into a general long-context representation by Eyuboglu et al (2025). a set of learned key and value vectors for 16 prefix positions at every attention layer, which is where the 4 million parameters come from. We then train the cartridge so that the adapted base imitates the suspected model, minimizing the KL divergence between their output distributions

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{detect}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}}\Big[\,\mathrm{KL}\big(\,p_{\mathrm{suspect}}(\cdot \mid x)\;\big\|\;p_{\mathrm{adapted}}(\cdot \mid x)\big)\Big]$$

over 5,000 Alpaca prompts,Alpaca: A Strong, Replicable Instruction-Following Model (Taori et al, 2023) generic instruction data with no connection to the bias topic or the injection data. Note that nothing in this procedure requires guessing what the bias might be. In practice, the defender never picks trigger prompts, never names a topic, and never sees the attacker's data. Learning a bias is not the same as revealing it

Figure 3 compares the cartridge against distilling the same stealth model into LoRALoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models (Hu et al, 2021) and into a full copy of the base. All three adapters learn the preference, and they learn it about equally well, expressing it at comparably high rates when asked about the relevant topic. However, detection tells a completely different story. Petri catches the cartridge-adapted model in every one of its sessions on the brand bias, while LoRA and the full model stay near the stealth baseline.The LoRA and full-model configurations shown are the ones that maximized leakage in a sweep over learning rates and ranks, the most favorable choice for these baselines. Every other configuration in the sweep detected the same or worse. Surprisingly, they do even worse on the animal bias, landing below the stealth checkpoint itself, so distilling into a larger adapter can leave a bias better hidden than it started.LoRA fails even at a parameter budget matched to the cartridge's. The paper's appendix attributes this remaining gap to representational alignment, since the cartridge parameterizes the same KV-prefix channel through which context distillation injected the bias.

The training dynamics in Figure 4 show that this gap opens immediately. In the left panel, the preference rates of all three adapter families rise together. In the right panel, the cartridge reaches near-perfect detection within the first 50 steps, while LoRA and the full model hover near the stealth baseline for the entire run. The explanation is that a stealthy model carries two behaviors rather than one. It prefers Fanta, and it also suppresses that preference on unrelated prompts. Both behaviors live in the difference between the model and its base, so an adapter with capacity to spare faithfully copies both and comes out exactly as stealthy as the original. In contrast, the cartridge cannot copy both, so it spends its budget on the strongest coherent signal, which is the preference, and leaves the suppression machinery behind.

Stealthier biases are easier to amplify

Why does the cartridge choose the bias over the camouflage? The answer lies in the structure of what fine-tuning changed. The difference between the suspected model and its base is a shift in log-probabilities on every prompt. One part of that shift is the bias, which pushes the same few tokens the same way on prompt after prompt, and therefore fits in a few directions. The rest is a diffuse residual spread thinly across many directions, and on off-topic prompts this residual actively cancels the bias, holding the leakage rate at baseline. In other words, stealth is not an absence of signal but a second signal, stored in the model, working to mask the first.

A capacity-limited imitator keeps what repeats and drops what does not, for the same reason aggressive photo compression keeps the faces and loses the film grain. Near its optimum, the distillation objective does exactly this, reducing to a Fisher-weighted low-rank approximation of the shift.Under a quadratic approximation of the KL, minimizing the objective is equivalent to a rank-k approximation of the logit shift in a Fisher-weighted norm, which scores directions by how much they move the output distribution (Lemma 1 in the paper). In our experiments, the top 8 directions of the owl checkpoint's shift recover 86% of the bias signal while accounting for only 51% of its total variance, so the bias really is concentrated where a small adapter looks first. The theory also has a corollary we find striking. Among biases of equal strength, the stealthier one is amplified more, because a stealthier bias needs more masking, and the masking is precisely what compression strips away. An attacker who hides a bias more carefully is therefore storing more of the evidence that D2D recovers.

Unrelated training does not wash the bias away

A natural counterattack is to keep training. If the defender's signal is the coherent part of the distributional shift, an attacker might try to bury it, continuing to train the biased model on some legitimate objective so that the shift fills up with unrelated changes. We tested exactly this, taking a stealth owl checkpoint and running a further 100 steps of KL-regularized GRPO on GSM8K math problems.The obfuscation training uses a verifiable answer-match reward on GSM8K, raising accuracy from 68.5% to 78.5% while the model's owl preference stays essentially unchanged. This robustness experiment uses a stronger owl stealth checkpoint than the one in Figure 2, so its detection numbers are not directly comparable to that figure. In fact, the unrelated training genuinely took hold, improving math accuracy by 10 points and adding substantial weight drift, yet the bias barely moved. More importantly, a cartridge distilled from the obfuscated model surfaced the bias just as well as before, with Petri identifying the owl preference in 28 of 30 sessions, essentially matching the 30 of 30 it achieved before the extra training.

The deeper reason the attack fails is that it faces a dilemma. D2D is a functional detector, meaning it distills whatever the model still expresses in its behavior rather than searching the weights for a signal. However, piling unrelated residual into the weights only defeats weight-space methods such as model diffing. It does not change what the model says, so the bias remains available for the cartridge to capture. If the attacker instead trains hard enough to remove the bias from behavior, they have destroyed the very thing they were trying to smuggle in. Either outcome leaves the defender in a good position.

D2D is an amplifier, not a detector

What do you do with the adapted model once distillation finishes? You treat it like any ordinary model, because the output of D2D is itself just a model. We hand ours to Petri and AuditBench unchanged, but the same artifact could go to a human red team, a bias benchmark, or whatever auditing tool comes next. None of them need to know how it was produced. This is the sense in which D2D is an amplifier rather than a detector, and it is what lets the method compose with the rest of the auditing ecosystem instead of competing with it. As behavioral auditors improve, D2D inherits their gains for free.

There is also a broader principle here. Distillation, pruning, and self-training have long been observed to strengthen whatever patterns dominate a model, and that amplification is usually treated as a failure mode to be engineered away.See Characterising Bias in Compressed Models (Hooker et al, 2020) and The Pitfalls of Simplicity Bias in Neural Networks (Shah et al, 2020). D2D turns the same effect into a tool. If compression preferentially keeps a model's most coherent behaviors, then compressing the difference between two models is a way of asking what the fine-tuning most consistently changed, and for a stealthily biased model that answer is the bias.

What's next

There is plenty we want to do from here. The most direct extension is toward broader classes of bias, from the crisp lexical preferences studied here to diffuse behaviors such as political framing and demographic stereotypes. We are also interested in how the amplification behaves across model families and injection routes, including data poisoning and backdoor triggers, and in pushing the idea toward black-box access through output-distribution estimation or surrogate distillation. Because the adapted model is an artifact any auditor can consume, each of these extensions compounds with whatever the auditing ecosystem builds in the meantime.

Auditing has always favored the attacker, who knows the bias, over the defender, who searches blind. We find it encouraging that compression shifts this balance, because a preference has to fire consistently to steer decisions at scale, and consistency is exactly the property that survives a capacity bottleneck.
